Life Blueprint: How to Design and Plan the Future You Want

Quick Rundown: You're ambitious. You want to escape the 9–5 grid. Excellent. This week, we'll craft a plan that helps you get unstuck so you can start crushing your goals.

Andrew Folts

Apr 15, 2019 at 2:38am

Last week, I kicked off a fifteen-part series to answer one burning question: how the hell do I create a business that allows me to quit my job and start living the life I want? A lot of people want to know the answer to that question—but, frankly, I don't think we're going to get it from finance gurus or millionaire "lifestyle" coaches. We need to go out and earn the solution through curiosity, gutsiness, and hard work.

Are you game for that? Alright, let's do this.

If we're going to fuck with the lines, if we're going to chase true freedom, we need a solid plan—a blueprint for the life we want to create.

Part 1: Preparation

What we're about to do is not easy. It's going to take both time and emotional energy, so you need to carve out some space in your life. If you try to create a plan for change while surrounded by distractions, you're going to end up right back where you started.

So break out the camping gear, book a motel, or take a drive to your local park. You should dedicate, at minimum, 4–8 hours to go through the process and allow any insights to fully percolate. If you can spare a whole weekend you should, but work with what you've got.

Also, make sure you've taken care of any prior commitments before embarking on your mini sabbatical. The last thing you want is to be stressing the whole time or to get interrupted half way through. Turn your phone off—better yet, leave it at home.

We're going to be doing a fair amount of writing, so you'll want to pick the medium that works best for you. I'm a fan of writing in a journal or on loose paper, but you can draft your blueprint in Word, Google Docs, or even Notion if you're more digitally inclined. Just make sure you get all this stuff out of your head and onto a page.

To recap...

  • Set aside 4–8 hours (ideally, a weekend)

  • Get FAR away from distractions

  • Take care of any commitments beforehand

  • Choose the writing tools that work the best for you

Part 2: Knowing Yourself

First, before galavanting off to set some crazy-ambitious goals, you need to know who you are. "Me? I know who I am!" Ok, Robert Downey Jr. in Tropic Thunder, you may THINK you know, but let's just be sure so we don't build this blueprint on false assumptions.

Making Your Flow List

What we're going to do is create a list of 20–30 "flow moments." These are times in your life when you felt the most free—when creativity and happiness came effortlessly, and you were completely in the moment.

They don't have to be in any specific order, just write down whatever comes to mind first (your strongest memories). Here are a few of mine:

  • Coming up with the idea for F the Lines

  • Stargazing under a blanket with a girl in college

  • Talking with a mentor about our creative aspirations

Finding Your Archetypes

Now that you've made your list, the next step is assigning an "archetype" to each memory. Don't worry, we're not going to get all wu wu here, the goal is just to define the different aspects of your identity—what motivates you?

Technically, psychiatrist Carl Jung came up with the twelve archetypes we'll be using, but you'll recognize them as characters from movies, books, stories, etc, and even people in your day-to-day life. They're pretty self-explanatory, but if you want to dive deeper, The Hero and The Outlaw by Carol Pearson is a great read. I also created a handy table with all the key points.

Ok, onward!

For each item on your flow list, write down which of the following archetypes most closely fits that memory. You can only pick ONE archetype per memory.

  • Ruler: exert control

  • Creator: craft something new

  • Sage: understand your world

  • Innocent: retain or renew faith

  • Explorer: maintain independence

  • Outlaw: break the rules

  • Hero: act courageously

  • Magician: affect transformation

  • Jester: have a good time

  • Regular Guy/Gal: be ok just as you are

  • Lover: find and give love

  • Caregiver: care for others

Ranking Your Archetypes

Alright, now make a tally of your top archetypes and sort that list according to the number of flow moments. Here's what mine looks like:

  • Creator: 5

  • Lover: 4

  • Explorer: 3

  • Sage: 3

  • Magician: 2

  • Jester: 1

  • Hero: 1

Defining What You Stand For

Excellent. So you know which archetypes make up your collective identity, but what do they actually mean to you? What beliefs and values will you stand for when the going gets rough and you need to make big decisions?

For each of your top five archetypes (the fewer the better), write three words that describe how you will implement it in your life. This is how I mapped out my "Creator" archetype:

  • Minimalism: using the fewest resources possible

  • Sustainability: creating a better future vs indulging short-term

  • Originality: giving new ideas to the world

Finished with your list? Nice job.

Crafting a Life Statement

The point of all this isn't just to understand your personality type—you can do that with a BuzzFeed quiz. (Don't click that link, we've got work to do...I'm testing you!) What we want to do is figure out which parts of our identity are in conflict.

I've been working eighty-hour weeks for the past few years, hustling to build a freelance business, and I let my love life fall by the wayside. I thought that was fine, but, (judging by my own list) it's clearly not, and I need to find a way to get my need for independence (The Explorer) to mesh with my desire for intimacy (The Lover).

The best way to iron out cognitive dissonance is to write a sentence—it will literally force you bring conflicting words together. Think of it as a positioning statement for your life. You don't need to get fancy, just identify the problem and write a straightforward solution that incorporates your top archetypes. Here's what I came up with for Creator, Lover, Explorer, Sage, Magician:

I am a creator who seeks out deep intimacy with other open-minded people and cultivates freedom through shared knowledge and inspiration.

I've now made intimacy a priority and attached it to a mission of "seeking" open-minded people, which is basically a target audience. I'm also going to focus on becoming more free and helping other people do the same, which feels way less boring than trying to "network more" or "go on more dates."

What did you come up with? Tweet it to me!

Part 3: Setting Goals

You're still with me. You didn't click on that BuzzFeed quiz and get sidetracked like Sir Robin at the Bridge of Death. Good. We'll go far together.

Now that you know your archetypes and you've distilled those archetypes into a pithy life statement, it's time to set some goals. Ideally, we want to filter these goals into as few "ventures" as humanly possible to focus our efforts.

You probably have a lot of ventures going already. For example, I'm a drummer. I'd love to go on tour and release an album, but my #1 priority right now is figuring out how to create a business that sets me free—I need to concentrate on that goal.

If you can find a way to bundle multiple ventures together, great. Otherwise, the less important ones need to go on the back burner. And believe me when I say: you must be ruthless in cutting out anything that's not essential to your mission, because it will take more time and energy than you can possibly imagine. Here's where I landed:

  1. F the Lines: create a "freedom business"

  2. Sherpa Design Co.: make money in the short-term

  3. Relationships: meet people, seek out intimacy

Defining Your Ventures

For each of these ventures, you're going to write two paragraphs. In the first, you're going to describe "Where I am." This is a no-bullshit analysis of your present situation. What have you accomplished? What are you struggling with? What are you unsure about? What roadblocks are stopping you from moving forward?

This is an abridged version of what I wrote for my F the Lines venture:

Where I Am: I've posted a few articles, I but haven't officially launched yet. I don't have a following, or even know whether F the Lines will be interesting/helpful to other people. My website isn't finished, and I need to record an intro video/podcast to launch the project. I'm interested in writing a book and possibly doing some coaching, but I'm not entirely sure how I'm going to make money.

In the second paragraph, you're going to write down "Where I want to be." Paint a picture of the exact life you want—limit your thinking to the next 1–10 years. I'm keeping my examples brief, but the more defined you can make your ambitions, the more likely you are to succeed. Go to town—really let your imagination run wild. Remember...

Fuzzy goals = fuzzy results

Where I Want to Be: I want to start a podcast and interview other creative entrepreneurs. I want to start making some money on Medium to verify that people actually get value from my content. I want to transition to FTL full time by the end of 2020. I want to help other creatives learn how to build a "freedom business" that allows them to escape the 9–5 world or unsatisfying service industry jobs. I want to buy a travel van and live on the road.

Next, extract any major milestones from your paragraph and turn them into a bulleted list under each venture. Don't worry about the order yet, we're going to get to that in just a second. Some of the goals I wrote down for F the Lines...

  • Make $100,000/year

  • Buy and convert a Ford Transit Connect van

  • Go on a "van life" road trip to Maine

  • Start a podcast and interview creative entrepreneurs

  • Offer business and life coaching sessions

Prioritizing Your Goals

Alright, my friend. We're in the home stretch. I borrowed this last technique from 7 Strategies for Wealth & Happiness by Jim Rohn. The book is a bit cheesy, but the concepts are pure gold, and you should read it if you want a quick way to learn more about goal-setting and personal finance.

Take a look at each goal for your three ventures and write down when you think you can accomplish it by. Don't worry about guessing wrong—you can always adjust later, but the important part is to get some dates down.

Once you've done that, split the goals up by timeframe and reorder them by importance. For example: one month, six months, one year, two years, five years, etc. It can help to do this part in an Excel spreadsheet or, ideally, an app like Notion that allows you to set reminders. That way, you can quickly reorder your goals, instead of rewriting them over and over.

Take a look at each chunk of time. If you have a lot of goals in the "One Month" section, but only a few in "One Year" and "Five Years," you might need to revisit your "Where I Want to Be" paragraphs and spend some more time describing the life you're trying to create.

If you have a shortage of short-term goals, that's ok. We're going to cover creating a productivity system and mapping out fine-grained tasks in the next two articles of the Framework Journey series.
