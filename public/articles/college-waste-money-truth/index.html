College is a Huge Waste of Money. Or is it? Here's the Truth.

Quick Rundown: What if I told that there’s this THING you absolutely must do. It costs $50,000, four years of your life, and may or may not leave you better off.

Andrew Folts

Mar 25, 2019 at 12:03am

What if I told that there’s this THING you absolutely must do. It costs $50,000, four years of your life, and may or may not leave you better off than when you started. Am I describing a Ponzi scheme or college?

Well, that’s the problem, isn’t it? Maybe both.

From the time we were kids, everyone—family, friends, government, the media—has been telling us that we have to get a college education. But, in all honesty, before I started my first semester at Ithaca in 2009, I cannot recall a single person ever arguing against college, or even urging me to consider other options.

If you’re thinking about going to college or know someone who is, my advice is to step back RIGHT NOW, and start treating the decision like any other major purchase, not a settled fact.

I say this because higher education in America is completely backwards.

Every day, we ask teenagers (as young as 15 or 16) to make monumental decisions about their future—involving sums of debt they cannot possibly be expected to understand and career paths they often have little real-world experience with.

Reverse Education

Countries like Germany take a more sensible approach by putting an emphasis on apprenticeships that cultivate practical skills, which can help teenagers make more informed decisions about their education, or circumvent college entirely through vocational tracks.

After landing my first full-time job at an advertising agency in New Jersey, it didn’t take long before I started to realize just how few of those practical skills (business sense, financial literacy, etc) I had developed at college—how much of my experience was bloated with abstract knowledge, completely untested by real world challenges and responsibilities.

In the one year I spent at that agency, I learned more about businesses (and life, in general) than I had in eight semesters at multiple well-respected schools. Running my own freelance design business over the course of the next two years was even more helpful—and these two experiences ultimately led me to begin feeling like college had, overall, been a very expensive waste of time.

Experience before expense

Now, I don’t mean college itself is a waste of time. Quite the opposite.

Today, more than ever, it’s crucial for young people to be exposed to a wide range of subjects, cultures, and skill sets. But too often, the experiences that help us understand what information is important and how we’re going to use it comes after college, when it should be coming before.

No one should be paying $50,000/year to “figure out what they want to do with their life.”

With that in mind, whether you’re looking at colleges or helping someone else make a decision, I’m going to give you some thoughts based on my own mistakes and successes.

How to make a smart investment in your education

These by no means are meant to be the end-all-be-all of going to college, but they can set you in the right direction, so you can commit with more confidence that the investment will be worth it.

Be an entrepreneur: A solid business sense is one of the hardest things to develop in college. So, start your own company—this could be as simple as lawn mowing or fixing basic computer problems for people in your neighborhood. Running your own operation will teach you about managing money and expenses, dealing with customers, and marketing yourself, which are more important than almost anything else you can learn.

Get real world experience: I’ve heard horror stories from friends who made it all the way through college without ever doing any real work in the field they majored in, then ending up hating it. The moral of the story is that you should always get an internship, volunteer, or work part-time before you make a huge investment in your education. This will help you find a more reliable career fit, plus you’ll have a much clearer sense of what’s important to learn in school, because you already know how you’re going to put it to use.

Take courses online: These could be official courses offered by a university or learning platforms like Treehouse and Skillshare. Either way, these are inexpensive ways to get ahead and give yourself a better sense of what kinds of subjects you’ll enjoy.

Find a mentor: Frankly, parents and counselors can provide terrible advice because they’re too close to you. They have ulterior that may not be in your best interest, even if they’re well-intentioned.  Look for someone who has a lot of experience and can assess your goals from a fresh point of view. Since they’ve “been through it all before,” a good mentor will support you in navigating a career and life, in general—plus, they might even be able to suggest some great alternatives you’ve never considered.
