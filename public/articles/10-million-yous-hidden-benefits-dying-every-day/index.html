10 Million You's: The Hidden Benefits of Dying Every Day

Quick Rundown: Making the right decisions every day is hard. A simple trick you can easily implement is dividing each moment into different "selves," aka dying.

Andrew Folts

Apr 2, 2019 at 2:59am

It was the sushi that killed me. In that first bite, I suddenly realized that I was dead. And not only that—I was about to die again.

Ok, that intro was super-harsh. Let me rewind a bit.

Theseus

While I was going to Ithaca for my first two years of college, I developed this addiction to philosophy. Even then, I knew it was pretty much useless (in the sense of directly repaying my $50k of student loan debt). But I couldn't help myself.

I was particularly interested in thought experiments: outlandish scenarios that philosophers devise to illustrate a certain problem or theory. One of my all-time favorites is the Ship of Theseus. It goes something like this:

Suppose a ship sailed by the Greek hero Theseus is kept in a harbor as a museum piece. Over time, some of the wooden parts begin to rot—and after a century or so, all of the parts have been replaced. Is it still the "same" ship?

This is a direct analogy to human identity, aka: "Am I the same person I was ten years ago? Will I be the same person in twenty years?" The comfortable answer is yes, but I'm going to ask you to do something strange. I'm going to ask you to start dying with me. Ready? Let's do this.

The Day of a Thousand Deaths

Back to the sushi. I don't normally make profound existential discoveries while eating seaweed and rice, but on this particular Friday, I was late to work, and those sushi rolls were literally the only thing standing between me and a four-hour shift of selling shoes on an empty stomach.

Back then (ok, you got me...it was like a year ago), I was cooking spaghetti for 50% of my dinners and rarely making more than a single serving at a time, so it was a pleasant surprise to find myself pre-stocked for once.

In that moment, I felt a kind of gratitude—not to "myself," but to a former version of myself who had spent time to make the sushi. That Andrew (yesterday at 5pm Andrew) would never get to enjoy the fruits of his labor. He had sacrificed short-term pleasure to make life better for a future self.

It was weirdly heroic. And that feeling of heroism (I'll call it "self-patriotism") changed how I looked at my life. By separating myself into many "selves" that were constantly dying and being born in every moment, I gained a deeper respect for how my decisions affected the person I would become.

Like Ryan Gosling in Blade Runner 2049, when you decide to start giving up meaningless in-the-moment temptations, it allows you to shift your entire life into a higher gear—to live for a greater purpose.

Dying for the right cause is the most human thing we can do.

Becoming a Self-Patriot

I think you'll probably agree that it's easier to advocate for someone else than to stand up for yourself. I'm reminded of this video, where Maryia Bulka (an artist/designer) is struggling to raise her prices.

Chris Do (a business coach) asks her to pretend she's selling his services, instead. You can probably guess what happens. She instantly becomes more proactive and confident, simply because she's advocating for someone other than herself.

Chris sums it up nicely:

So, you're more brave for an imaginary relationship that doesn't even exist. Spend some of that energy on yourself. You're worth it.

That lesson can be applied to our multi-selves strategy. What if every time you binge-watched Netflix instead of getting some work done, the side-effects happened to a friend instead of just to you. Literally picture it: you go on a five-episode tear: they're exhausted and depressed the next day. You resist the urge: they wake up feeling totally energized. Wouldn't that change the decisions you made?

To practice, pick one bad habit that's weighing you down. Imagine how shitty your future self will feel if you do that thing. Even better, say it out loud. How is it going to make them feel? What thoughts will be running through their head? How will they react? Now consider the opposite. What if you make the right choice? How will that affect your future self?

Ok, we're making progress. Now that you've created an emotional connection to one of your future selves, you can apply the same process to other parts of your life. I'll give you a few examples.

Ex. 1: Decaffinating

I love coffee. When I was working as a copywriter in New Jersey, I used to drink 3–4 cups just to make it through the day. It gives you energy, right? Nope. In fact, all caffeine really does is prevent the chemical adenosine from binding with your "adenosine receptors"—in other words, it just delays sleepiness. Eventually, that sleepiness will catch up with you, making the effects of caffeine completely pointless.

Knowing this wasn't enough to get me to stop drinking coffee. So, I set up a simple experiment to see how coffee actually affected me. A few weeks after going cold turkey, I checked back on my progress. The results were pretty obvious. From February 10th onward, my productivity started to increase dramatically.

Oftentimes, just knowing that something is bad for you isn't enough to outweigh the short-term appeal. Setting a timeline and measuring the actual results can give you extra ammo in your fight for positive change.

Ex. 2: Thinking Rich

"What can I afford?" is a weird question. Weird, because it relates not just to how much money you currently have, but how much you think you will have in the future. You might say to yourself: "I want to become a professional photographer. I could buy a higher-end camera right now, but I can't afford to because it will wipe out my savings, so I'll just buy a cheap one."

There are two problems with this. One, you're going to become dissatisfied with the cheap camera pretty quickly as your skills develop; when you outgrow it and buy the higher-end camera, it will cost you 50% more than if you just bought a more expensive camera to begin with. Two, the things you surround yourself with affect your confidence. If you use a better camera (wear better clothes, eat better food, etc), you will start feeling and acting like a better person—just make sure you're investing in things that truly matter to you.

It can help to think about the future self you want to become, then track backwards. What did they do to get to where they are? What products were essential to their success. Would a expert photographer buy a sub-par camera just to save a few bucks? Hell no. They would buy the best camera possible: one they could use for a long time and be inspired by on a daily basis.

Bringing it All Together

Following any of the individual pieces of advice we just covered won't get you very far. But when you use them together, they actually become pretty damn powerful. Let's recap:

1. Imagine your future self

Pick a habit you'd like to start or stop. Describe (in as much detail as you possibly can) how the action you take will affect your future self. Think about what your "past selves" have given you and what you can give that future self.

2. Make the results measurable

You need to prove to yourself that changing this habit will have a positive impact on your life. Setting a timeline (say, two weeks) and tracking a specific effect (productivity, stress, etc) is a great way to motivate yourself.

3. Act more successful than you are

Reverse-engineer your successful future self: how did they get to where they are? What investments or decisions contributed most to their growth? Make a list of 5–10 items, then rewrite the list, ordering them by importance.
